'''Dubitative mood''' ([[list of glossing abbreviations|abbreviated]] {{sc|'''dub'''}}) is an [[epistemic modality|epistemic]] [[grammatical mood]] found in some languages, that indicates that the statement is dubious, doubtful, or uncertain.{{cite web|url=http://www.sil.org/linguistics/GlossaryOflinguisticTerms/WhatIsDubitativeMood.htm|title=What is dubitative mood?|publisher=SIL International|work=Glossary of linguistic terms|last=Loos|first=Eugene E.|coauthors=Susan Anderson; Dwight H. Day, Jr.; Paul C. Jordan; J. Douglas Wingate|accessdate=2009-12-28}} It may subsist as a separate morphological category, as in [[Bulgarian language|Bulgarian]], or else as a category of use of another form, as of the [[conditional mood]] of [[Italian language|Italian]]. An example can be taken from [[Ojibwe language|Ojibwe]], an [[Algonquian languages|Algonquian language]] of North America. Verbs in Ojibwe can be marked with a dubitative suffix, indicating that the speaker is doubtful or uncertain about what they are saying. So ''aakozi'' means "he is sick," while ''aakozidog'' can be translated as something like "he must be sick; I guess he's sick; maybe he's sick; he might be sick."{{cite book|url=http://www.edu.gov.on.ca/eng/document/curricul/ojibwe.pdf|format=PDF|series=The Ontario Curriculum|title=Native Languages: Ojibwe and Cree|publisher=Ontario Ministry of Education|isbn=0-7794-3384-X}} ==References== {{Grammatical moods}} [[Category:Grammatical moods]] {{ling-morph-stub}} [[nl:Dubitatief]]
