{{Notability|date=November 2007}} '''Organizational Intelligence''' (OI) is the capability of an organization to comprehend and conclude knowledge relevant to its business purpose. * an ability to make sense of complex situations and act effectively * an ability to interpret and act upon relevant events and signals in the environment * ability to develop, share and use knowledge relevant to its business purpose * ability to reflect and learn from experience OI embraces both [[knowledge management]] (KM) and [[organizational learning]], as it is the application of KM concepts to a business environment, additionally including learning mechanisms, comprehension models and business value network models, such as the [[balanced scorecard]] concept. OI's focus includes the creation, fostering and management of organizational competencies (OCs). Organizational intelligence has been defined as "the capacity to sense, make sense, and act in flexible, creative, adaptive ways",{{Citation | last = McMaster | first = Michael D. | title = Organizational Intelligence | publisher = Paradigm Shift | pages = | date = December 12, 1998 | url = http://www.parshift.com/Speakers/Speak011.htm | accessdate = 6 February 2010 }} as "collaborative problem-solving between people and technical artefacts within and beyond complex enterprises"{{Cite book | last = Veryard | first = Richard | authorlink = Richard Veryard | coauthors = | title = Component-Based Business | publisher = Springer | date = 2001 | location = London | pages = | url = | doi = | id = | isbn = 1-852-33361-8}} and as "how well people put their heads together in a group, team, organization, or community".{{Citation | last = Bucuvalas | first = Abigail | title = Studying King Arthur's Round Table: An Interview with Professor David Perkins | publisher = Harvard Graduate School of Education | pages = | date = November 1, 2003 | url = http://www.gse.harvard.edu/news/features/perkins11012003.html | accessdate = 6 February 2010 }} [[Harold Wilensky]] argued that organizational intelligence benefited from healthy argument and constructive rivalry.{{Citation | last = Gladwell | first = Malcolm | author-link = Malcolm Gladwell | title = Connecting the Dots | newspaper = New Yorker | pages = | date = March 10, 2003 | url = http://gladwell.com/2003/2003_03_10_a_dots.html | accessdate = 3 February 2010 }} ==References== *[[James G.
