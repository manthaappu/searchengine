{{Electronic structure methods}} The '''[[muffin tin|muffin-tin]] approximation''' is a shape approximation of the [[potential field]] in an atomistic environment. It is most commonly employed in [[quantum mechanical]] simulations of [[electronic band structure]] in [[Solid-state physics|solids]]. The approximation was proposed by [[John C. Slater]]. {{cite book |first=Feng |last=Duan |coauthors=Guojun, Jin |date=2005 |title=Introduction to Condensed Matter Physics |publisher=[[World Scientific]] |location=Singapore |volume=1 |isbn=9789812387110 }} {{cite journal |last=Slater |first=J. C. |date=1937 |title=Wave Functions in a Periodic Potential |journal=[[Physical Review]] |volume=51 |pages=846–851 |doi=10.1103/PhysRev.51.846}} Many modern electronic structure methods employ the approximation. {{cite book |author=Kaoru Ohno, Keivan Esfarjani, Yoshiyuki |year=1999 |title=Computational Materials Science Physical Properties of Solids |page=349 |publisher=[[Springer (publisher)|Springer]] |isbn=3540672389 }} In its simplest form, non-overlapping spheres are centered on the atomic positions. Within these regions, the [[Screening effect|screened potential]] experienced by an electron is approximated to be spherically symmetric about the given nucleus. In the remaining interstitial region, the potential is approximated as a constant. Continuity of the potential between the atom-centered spheres and interstitial region is enforced. In the interstitial region of constant potential, the single electron wave functions can be expanded in terms of [[plane wave]]s. In the atom-centered regions, the wave functions can be expanded in terms of [[spherical harmonic]]s and the [[eigenfunction]]s of a radial [[Schrödinger equation]]. {{cite journal |last=Slater |first=J. C. |date=1937 |title=An Augmented Plane Wave Method for the Periodic Potential Problem |journal=[[Physical Review]] |volume=92 |pages=603–608 |doi=10.1103/PhysRev.92.603 }} Such use of functions other than plane waves as basis functions is termed the augmented plane-wave approach (of which there are many variations).
