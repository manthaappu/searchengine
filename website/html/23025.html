[[Image:7360 pinout.png|thumb|right|300px|TED pinout]] The '''7360 Text Editing Device''' ('''TED''') was an [[integrated circuit]] made by [[MOS Technology|MOS Technology, Inc.]] It was a [[Video Display Controller|video chip]] that also contained [[Sound chip|sound generation]] hardware, [[Dynamic random access memory|DRAM]] refresh circuitry, [[Programmable Interval Timer|interval timers]], and [[computer keyboard|keyboard]] input handling. It was designed for the [[Commodore International|Commodore]] [[Commodore Plus/4|Plus/4]] and [[Commodore 16|16]]. Packaging consisted of a [[JEDEC]]-standard 48-pin [[Dual in-line package|DIP]]. ==Video capabilities== The video capabilities provided by the TED were largely a subset of those in the [[MOS Technology VIC-II|VIC-II]]. The TED supported five video modes: * Text mode of 40×25 characters with 8×8 pixels * Multicolor text (4×8 pixels per character, double pixel width in x direction) * Extended background color mode (8×8 pixels per character) * Multicolor Graphics 160×200 pixels * Hi-Res Graphics 320×200 pixels These were largely unchanged from the corresponding VIC-II modes. However, the TED lacked the [[Sprite (computer science)|sprite]] capabilities of the VIC-II, making it unsuitable for most [[Action game|action]] [[Computer game|games]]. The TED did include one feature that the VIC-II lacked: [[luminance]] control. Fifteen of its 16 colors (black being the exception) could be assigned one of 8 luminance values, thus making the TED capable of displaying a far wider array of colors than the VIC-II. The first channel produced a [[square wave]], and the second could produce either a square wave or [[white noise]]. This tone generator was designed for business applications, and did not provide the extensive sound features found in the [[MOS Technology SID|SID]] chip. ==Other features== The TED includes three [[16-bit]] interval timers, which consist of [[Counter|down counters]] operating at the master clock frequency. They can generate [[Interrupt request|IRQs]] on underflow. The chip also contains an [[I/O port]], which is used on the Plus/4 and 16 to scan the keyboard and [[joystick]]. In addition, it handles [[bank switching]], used by the operating system to maximize the amount of [[Random access memory|RAM]] available to [[Commodore BASIC]]. An undesirable feature of the chip is its well-known tendency to destroy itself though overheating.
