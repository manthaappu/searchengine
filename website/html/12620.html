Commission |court=CFI |SubmitDate=7 June |SubmitYear=2004 |DecideDate=17 September |DecideYear=2007 |FullName=Microsoft Corp. v Commission of the European Communities |CelexID=62004A0201 |CaseType=Action for annulment, Appeal against penalty |CaseNumber=T-201/04 |Chamber=Grand chamber |Nationality=[[United States]] |Procedural= |Ruling= |JudgeRapporteur= |JudgePresident=Bo Vesterdorf |Judge1= |Judge2= |Judge3= |Judge4= |Judge5= |Judge6= |Judge7= |Judge8= |Judge9= |Judge10= |Judge11= |Judge12= |AdvocateGeneral= |InstrumentsCited= |LegislationAffecting= |Keywords= }} The '''European Union Microsoft competition case''' is a case brought by the [[European Commission]] of the [[European Union]] (EU) against [[Microsoft]] for abuse of its dominant position in the market (according to [[competition law]]). It started as a complaint from [[Novell]] over Microsoft's licensing practices in 1993, and eventually resulted in the EU ordering Microsoft to divulge certain information about its server products and release a version of [[Microsoft Windows]] without [[Windows Media Player]]. ==Initial complaints== In 1993, [[Novell]] said that Microsoft was blocking its competitors out of the market through anti-competitive practices. The complaint centered on the license practices at the time which required royalties from each computer sold by a supplier of Microsoft's operating system, whether or not the unit actually contained the Windows operating system. Microsoft reached a settlement in 1994, ending some of its license practices.{{cite news | url=http://www.news.com/2100-1023_3-204317.html | title=Microsoft investigated in Europe | first=Lamia | last=Abu-Haidar | publisher=[[CNET News.com]] | date=1997-10-16 | accessdate=2006-07-01}} [[Sun Microsystems]] joined the fray in 1998 when it complained about the lack of disclosure of some of the interfaces to Windows NT. The case widened even more when the EU started to look into how [[streaming media]] technologies were integrated with Windows.{{cite news | url=http://www.news.com/2100-1001_3-941090.html | title=EU looks to wrap up Microsoft probe | publisher=[[CNET News.com]] | first=Declan | last=McCullagh | authorlink=Declan McCullagh | date=2002-07-01 | accessdate=2006-07-01}} ==Judgment== Citing ongoing abuse by Microsoft, the EU reached a preliminary decision in the case in 2003 and ordered the company to offer both a version of Windows without Windows Media Player and the information necessary for competing networking software to interact fully with Windows desktops and servers.{{cite news | url=http://www.news.com/2100-1016_3-5060463.html | title=EU closes in on Microsoft penalty | publisher=[[CNET News.com]] | first=Ina | last=Fried | authorlink=Ina Fried | date=2003-08-06 | accessdate=2006-07-01}} In March 2004, the EU ordered Microsoft to pay [[Euro|€]]497 million ([[United States Dollar|$]]794 million or [[Pound sterling|£]]381 million), the largest fine ever handed out by the EU at the time, in addition to the previous penalties, which included 120 days to divulge the server information and 90 days to produce a version of Windows without Windows Media Player.[http://ec.europa.eu/comm/competition/antitrust/cases/decisions/37792/en.pdf Commission Decision of 24.03.2004 relating to a proceeding under Article 82 of the EC Treaty (Case COMP/C-3/37.792 Microsoft)]. Commission of the European Communities. Brussels, 21 April 2004.{{cite news | url=http://www.cnn.com/2004/BUSINESS/03/24/microsoft.eu/ | title=Microsoft hit by record EU fine | publisher=[[CNN]] | date=2004-03-24 | accessdate=2006-05-19 |archiveurl = http://web.archive.org/web/20060413082435/http://www.cnn.com/2004/BUSINESS/03/24/microsoft.eu/ |archivedate = 2006-04-13}}{{cite news | url=http://www.news.com/EU+slaps+record+fine+on+Microsoft/2100-1001_3-5178281.html | title=EU slaps record fine on Microsoft | publisher=[[CNET News.com]] | first=Michael | last=Parsons | coauthors=Best, Jo | date=2004-03-24 | accessdate=2006-07-01}} The next month Microsoft released a paper containing scathing commentary on the ruling including: "The commission is seeking to make new law that will have an adverse impact on intellectual property rights and the ability of dominant firms to innovate."{{cite news | url=http://www.news.com/2100-1016_3-5197390.html | title=Microsoft commentary slams EU ruling | publisher=[[CNET News.com]] | first=Ina | last=Fried | authorlink=Ina Fried | date=2004-04-21 | accessdate=2006-07-01}} Microsoft paid the fine in full in July 2004.{{cite news | url=http://www.news.com/2100-1014_3-5255715.html | title=Microsoft pays EU in full | publisher=[[CNET News.com]] | first=Matt | last=Hines | date=2004-07-02 | accessdate=2006-07-01}} In 2004, [[Neelie Kroes]] was appointed the European Commissioner for Competition; one of her first tasks was to oversee the fining brought onto Microsoft. Kroes has stated she believes [[open standards]] and [[open source]] are preferable to anything [[Proprietary software|proprietary]]:[http://blogs.zdnet.com/open-source/?p=2544 Open source as industrial policy] {{cquote|The Commission must do its part.....It must not rely on one vendor, it must not accept closed standards, and it must refuse to become [[Vendor lock-in|locked into a particular technology]] – jeopardizing maintenance of full control over the information in its possession}} ==Follow-up== Microsoft has a compliant version of its flagship operating system without Windows Media Player available under the negotiated name "[[Windows XP]] N."{{cite news | url=http://www.news.com/2100-1016_3-5960750.html | title=Still 'no demand' for media-player-free Windows | publisher=[[CNET News.com]] | first=Ingrid | last=Marson | date=2005-11-18 | accessdate=2006-07-01}} In response to the server information requirement, Microsoft released the [[source code]], but not the specifications, to [[Windows Server 2003]] service pack 1 to members of its Work Group Server Protocol Program (WSPP) on the day of the original deadline.{{cite news | url=http://open.itworld.com/4914/060125ms_ups_ante/page_1.html | title=Microsoft ups the ante with the E.C. | publisher=ITworld.com | first=Neil | last=Macehiter | date=2006-01-25 | accessdate=2006-07-01}} Microsoft also appealed the case, and the EU had a week-long hearing over it. [[Neelie Kroes]] stated:{{cite news | url=http://news.cnet.com/No-alternative-to-Microsoft-fine/2008-1014_3-6093104.html | title='No alternative' to Microsoft fine | publisher=[[CNET News.com]] | first=Dawn | last=Kawamoto | date=2006-07-12 | accessdate=2009-05-27}} {{cquote|Microsoft has claimed that its obligations in the decision are not clear, or that the obligations have changed. The EU threatened to increase the fine to €3 million ($4.81 million) per day on 31 July 2006 if Microsoft did not comply by then.{{cite news | url=http://www.zeropaid.com/bbs/archive/index.php/t-38334.html | title=EU fines Microsoft $357.3 million for defiance | publisher=[[Reuters]] | first=David | last=Lawsky | coauthors=Zawadzki, Sabina | date=2006-07-12 | accessdate=2006-07-12}} On 17 September 2007, Microsoft lost their appeal against the European Commission's case. The €497 million fine was upheld, as were the requirements regarding server interoperability information and bundling of Media Player. In addition, Microsoft has to pay 80% of the legal costs of the Commission, while the Commission has to pay 20% of the legal costs by Microsoft. However, the appeal court rejected the Commission ruling that an independent monitoring trustee should have unlimited access to internal company organization in the future.[http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/business/6998272.stm Microsoft loses anti-trust appeal]. On 22 October 2007, Microsoft announced that it would comply and not appeal the decision any more,[http://investing.reuters.co.uk/news/articleinvesting.aspx?type=tnBusinessNews&storyID=2007-10-22T114840Z_01_BRE001179_RTRIDST_0_BUSINESS-MICROSOFT-EU-DC.XML Microsoft finally bows to EU antitrust measures]. The interoperability information alone is available for a one-time fee of €10,000 (US$15,992).[http://www.itnews.com.au/News/63623,eu-forces-microsoft-to-cage-open-source-patent-dogs.aspx EU forces Microsoft to cage open source patent dogs]. itNews, 24 October 2007. On 27 February 2008, the EU fined Microsoft an additional €899 million (US$1.44 billion) for failure to comply with the March 2004 antitrust decision. This represents the largest penalty ever imposed in 50 years of EU competition policy. This latest decision follows a prior €280.5 million fine for non-compliance, covering the period from June 21, 2006 until October 21, 2007.[http://www.iht.com/articles/reuters/2008/02/27/business/OUKBS-UK-MICROSOFT-EU.php EU fines Microsoft 899 million] On 9 May 2008, Microsoft lodged an appeal in the European [[General Court (European Union)|Court of First Instance]] seeking to overturn the €899 million fine, officially stating that it intended to use the action as a "constructive effort to seek clarity from the court".[http://www.computerworld.com/action/article.do?command=viewArticleBasic&articleId=9084578&taxonomyId=14&intsrc=kc_top Update: Microsoft to appeal $1.3B EU fine] In its 2008 Annual Report Microsoft stated:http://news.cnet.com/8301-13505_3-10005379-16.html {{cquote|The European Commission closely scrutinizes the design of high-volume Microsoft products and the terms on which we make certain technologies used in these products, such as file formats, programming interfaces, and protocols, available to other companies. The Commission’s impact on product design may limit our ability to innovate in Windows or other products in the future, diminish the developer appeal of the Windows platform, and increase our product development costs.
