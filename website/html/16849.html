[[Image:Lifezones Pengo.svg|thumb|500px|Holdridge life zone classification scheme. Although conceived as three-dimensional by its originator, is usually shown as a two-dimensional array of hexagons in a triangular frame.]] The '''Holdridge life zones system''' is a global bioclimatic scheme for the classification of land areas. It was first published by [[Leslie Holdridge]] in 1947, and updated in 1967. It is a relatively simple system based on few empirical data, giving objective mapping criteria.[http://www.epa.gov/wed/pages/publications/abstracts/archived/lugo.htm Western Ecology Division | US EPA] A basic assumption of the system is that both [[soil type|soil]] and [[climax vegetation]] can be mapped once climate is known.http://links.jstor.org/sici?sici=0004-0851%28197322%295%3A3%3CA187%3ACOTAOT%3E2.0.CO%3B2-4&size=LARGE&origin=JSTOR-enlargePage While it was first designed for tropical and subtropical area, the system applies globally. The system has been shown to fit [[tropical]] vegetation zones, [[Mediterranean]] zones, and [[boreal ecosystem|boreal]] zones, but is less applicable to cold oceanic or cold arid climates where moisture becomes the determining factor. The system has found a major use in assessing the possible changes in natural vegetation patterns due to [[global warming]].[http://www.ngdc.noaa.gov/ecosys/cdroms/ged_iia/datasets/a06/reprints/lh1.htm] The three axis of the [[Barycentric subdivision]]s are: * [[precipitation]] (annual, logarithmic) * [[humidity]] provinces * [[potential evapotranspiration]] ratio (PET) to mean total annual precipitation. Further indicators incorporated into the system are: * [[biotemperature]] (mean annual, logarithmic) * [[Latitudinal|latitudinal regions]] * [[Altitudinal zonation|altitudinal belts]] Biotemperature is based on the growing season length and temperature. It is measured as the mean of refers to all temperatures above freezing, with all temperatures below freezing adjusted to 0 °C, as plants are dormant at these temperatures. Holdridge's system uses biotemperature first, rather than the temperate latitude bias of [[Clinton Hart Merriam|Merriam]]'s life zones, and does not primarily consider elevation.
